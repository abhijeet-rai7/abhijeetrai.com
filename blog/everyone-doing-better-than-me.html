Why Does It Feel Like Everyone Else Is Doing Better Than Me?

You open your phone and everyone is ahead. Better job, better relationship, better body, better life — all of it scrolling past while you sit exactly where you are. And under the envy there's something heavier: the quiet verdict that you're behind, that you wasted time, that something is wrong with you.

We've all been fed one sentence by every motivational voice: "They did it, so can you — just take the same steps." It sounds fair. It's also the source of most of your pain, and it isn't true.

No one starts on the same line. Look closely at anyone who's "ahead." Where were they born? What did their parents' confidence — or fear — pour into them before they could speak? Which teacher lifted them, or crushed them, in sixth grade? How much energy does their body give them? Who did they happen to sit beside on a train? What mood was the person in who interviewed them? Which year did the market rise under their feet? They chose none of it. Most of them can't even see it. But it decided more than their "steps" ever did.

Now turn it on yourself. The places you're "behind" grew from the same kind of causes you never chose either — your start, your wiring, your wounds, your luck.

So here's what quietly dissolves when you really see this: envy of the ones ahead, and contempt for yourself. Their win was never pure merit for you to resent. Your lag was never pure failure for you to be ashamed of. You've been comparing scores in a game where no two players were ever handed the same legs.

This isn't an excuse to stop trying. It's permission to stop bleeding — to put down a measuring stick that was always rigged. The relief is enormous, and it's honest.

And it opens a stranger, larger question: if you didn't author the person you became — your gifts, your gaps, all of it — then who is actually living this life you keep grading?
