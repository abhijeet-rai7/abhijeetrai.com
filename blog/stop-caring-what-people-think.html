How Do I Stop Caring So Much What People Think?

You replay the conversation for hours. You shrink yourself in rooms. You say yes when you mean no. There's an audience living in your head, and you perform for it all day — and it is exhausting.

Everyone tells you, "just stop caring what people think." As if you could flip a switch. You can't, because the advice never asks the real question: who, exactly, is being judged?

Sit with that. The "you" that trembles at the room's opinion — what is it, actually? Look for it. You'll find a picture of yourself: how you think you come across, the version of you that must be liked, approved, not laughed at. And where did that picture come from? It was built, piece by piece, out of other people's eyes — your parents', your classmates', strangers' — long before you were old enough to choose any of it. You didn't design the self you're so busy defending. It was installed.

Now notice two more things. First, the opinions you fear are happening inside other minds you cannot control or even see — you're terrified of weather in someone else's sky. Second, those opinions are about an image, not about the living thing reading these words right now. People judge the picture. The picture isn't you.

Freedom here isn't becoming confident enough to finally win everyone over — that's just performing harder. It's quieter than that: it's seeing that the small, separate self that needs all this approval can't actually be found when you look for it. There's no fixed thing inside that the judgment can land on. And what doesn't exist can't be wounded.

I won't pretend one essay ends a lifetime of people-pleasing. But this first honest look — who is being judged, and is it even real? — is where the audience in your head starts to lose its power over you.

And it opens the doorway my whole book walks through: if the self you defend all day was handed to you, and dissolves when you look straight at it… then what are you, underneath the performance?
