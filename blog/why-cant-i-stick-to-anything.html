Why Can't I Stick to Anything?

You've started so many times. Monday. The first of the month. The gym, the diet, the new routine, the better version of you. It holds for a week, maybe a month — and then it quietly collapses, and the same verdict lands: I have no willpower. Other people can do this. Something is wrong with me.

Before you sign that verdict again, look honestly at how a "decision" actually works.

Try this: decide, right now, to genuinely want something you feel nothing for. You can't. Wanting isn't something you switch on — it arrives on its own. And every choice you make rides on wanting. When the urge to skip the gym is heavier than the urge to go, the heavier one wins — and then a voice says "I chose to skip." It didn't choose. It reported.

You also didn't choose the wiring underneath all this: the childhood that set your habits, the stress that drains you, the way your particular brain hands out motivation or won't. "Willpower" isn't a virtue you were issued less of. It's a set of forces, almost none of which you picked.

So the guilt — "I'm lazy, I'm weak, I failed again" — stands on a mistake: that there's a free, separate you who could have just forced it and chose not to. Look for that person and you won't find them. There was only you, with the exact pulls you had, doing what those pulls produced.

Here's the strange part. Change doesn't usually begin with more self-punishment — that's just adding a heavier whip to a tired animal. It often begins the moment you stop whipping and actually see, clearly, how the whole machine of wanting and doing really works in you. Seeing replaces shame. And from that quieter place, things sometimes shift in a way force never managed.

I'm not selling you a productivity hack. I'm pointing at something underneath all of them: you were never the lone author who should have white-knuckled your way to a different life and failed. And if you weren't the author… who's been writing it?
