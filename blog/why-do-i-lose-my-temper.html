Why Do I Keep Losing My Temper?

It happens fast. Someone says the wrong thing, the traffic stops, a small thing tips you — and before you've decided anything, the heat is already up and the words are already out. Then, minutes later, the worst part lands: the regret. Why did I react like that again? What is wrong with me?

You've probably tried to "control" it — count to ten, breathe. Sometimes it works; often it doesn't, because by the time you remember the trick, the reaction has already fired.

Look honestly at the moment the anger comes. Did you choose it? Try, right now, to summon real fury on command, at nothing. You can't. Anger arrives on its own — this moment landing on old wounds, on a tired body, on a nervous system wired by years you didn't design. The flash comes first. Only afterward does a voice step in, either to ride it ("I'm right to be angry") or to regret it ("I shouldn't have").

This matters — and not as an excuse. If your anger hurt someone, the hurt is real and yours to repair. But the endless shame — I'm a bad person who can't control himself — stands on a belief that there was a calm, free you who chose to explode and could simply have chosen not to. In the half-second it fired, that "you" wasn't there. There were causes, then a reaction, then a commentator who claimed it.

Here's what shifts when you see this. Two things loosen. The shame afterward softens, because you stop sentencing a person who was never as free as the story claimed. And — strangely — the grip in the moment loosens too. Once you've seen, in calm moments, that anger is something that arises rather than something you are, the next time it rises you can catch it half a second earlier — as weather moving through, not as "me." That half-second is where everything changes.

You are not your temper. You are the one who notices it arrive.

And if the angry "you" isn't the real you — then who is? That's the question my book follows all the way down.
